The Last Light

The old lighthouse keeper climbed the spiral stairs for what he knew would be the last time. Forty-two years he had tended this beacon, watching over the rocky shore, guiding lost ships through treacherous waters in the dead of night.

Tomorrow, a new automated system would take over. No more would human hands adjust the great light, no more would someone stand vigil at the top of the tower.

Marcus reached the lamp room and looked out at the endless ocean. The sun was setting in brilliant shades of orange and gold, painting the water like liquid fire. He thought of all the ships he'd saved, the lives he'd touched without ever meeting them. Captains who'd seen his light and steered clear of danger. Families who'd made it home because of that faithful beam.

He struck a match and lit the lamp one final time, watching the great lens catch the flame and magnify it across the darkening sea. The light grew bright, steady, constant—just as it had every single night for four decades.

As darkness fell completely, the lighthouse beam swept across the water in its eternal rhythm. And though Marcus knew his time was ending, the light would continue. Changed, but still guiding. Still watching. Still saving.

He descended the stairs slowly, turning back only once to see the beacon burning bright against the night sky. Some lights, he thought, never truly go out.
